Short History

Dresden became the residence of the Saxon electors and kings and was famous for its baroque architecture and art collections. During the Second World War, severe bombing destroyed much of the historic center. In the GDR era, some ruins remained as memorials; after reunification, major projects such as the rebuilding of the Frauenkirche began. Today Dresden again presents itself as a cultural capital on the Elbe.

Culture & Lifestyle

Dresden offers classical concerts, opera, and galleries, but also modern street culture along the Neustadt district. The Elbe meadows serve as a green relaxation area, and festivals bring together local traditions and visitors. The mixture of old and new buildings invites discussion about memory, identity, and urban development in Germany.

Food Specialities

Dresdner Stollen

A rich Christmas fruit bread packed with raisins and dusted generously with powdered sugar, protected as a regional specialty of Dresden.

Eierschecke

A layered Saxon cake with a shortcrust base, a quark filling, and a golden custard-like topping — best enjoyed with a cup of coffee.

Sächsische Bratwurst

Hearty pan-fried sausages browned to a crisp in a cast-iron skillet, reflecting the robust flavours of traditional Saxon cuisine.

Interesting Facts

  • Dresden is sometimes called the "Florence on the Elbe" because of its art and architecture.
  • The Frauenkirche reconstruction used original stones that had been catalogued and stored for decades.
  • The city hosts the Striezelmarkt, one of Germany’s oldest Christmas markets, dating back to 1434.
  • Dresden lies close to the Elbe Sandstone Mountains (Sächsische Schweiz), a popular hiking region.
  • The Zwinger’s Kronentor is crowned by a replica of the Polish royal crown, symbolising Saxony’s historic union with Poland.
