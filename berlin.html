Short History

Berlin grew from a medieval trading town into the capital of Prussia and later the German Empire. In the 20th century it became a focal point of world history: the rise of National Socialism, destruction in the Second World War, and division into East and West during the Cold War. The fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989 and reunification in 1990 turned Berlin into a symbol of democratic renewal and European integration.

Culture & Lifestyle

Today Berlin is known for its creative scene, museums, and nightlife. Neighborhoods like Kreuzberg and Neukölln reflect a mix of immigrant cultures, while Museum Island and the Philharmonie showcase classical art and music. Daily life feels more relaxed than in many capitals: people cycle along the Spree, meet in parks like Tempelhofer Feld, and enjoy street food at weekly markets.

Food Specialities

Currywurst

Sliced sausage with spiced ketchup and curry powder, often served with fries. A classic Berlin street food that emerged after the war.

Döner Kebab

Introduced by Turkish immigrants, Döner has become one of Berlin’s most popular quick meals and reflects the city’s diversity.

Berliner Pfannkuchen

A jam‑filled doughnut enjoyed especially at New Year’s Eve and Carnival, though Berliners simply call it a Pfannkuchen.

Interesting Facts

  • Berlin has more bridges than Venice and numerous inner‑city lakes and canals.
  • The Berlin Wall stood for 28 years; today, segments like the East Side Gallery are open‑air museums.
  • There are over 170 museums in Berlin, ranging from ancient art to everyday life in the GDR.
  • Berlin’s public transport covers three fare zones and is a useful topic for transport‑related vocabulary.
