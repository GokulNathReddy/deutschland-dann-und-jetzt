Short History

Leipzig has been an important trade fair city since the Middle Ages, located at the crossroads of major routes. It became known for its publishing houses and universities, and for composers such as Bach and Mendelssohn. In the GDR, Leipzig was an industrial and cultural center. The Monday demonstrations of 1989, which started at St. Nicholas Church, played a major role in the peaceful revolution.

Culture & Lifestyle

Today Leipzig is popular with students and artists. The city offers classical music at the Gewandhaus and the Opera, but also alternative culture, street art, and independent cinemas. Old factories have been turned into studios and cultural spaces. Lakes around the city, created from former open-cast mines, provide recreation in summer.

Food & Garden Culture

Leipziger Lerche

Small shortcrust pastries filled with marzipan and nuts, topped with a cross-shaped pastry lid. Originally made with lark meat, they were reinvented after a hunting ban in 1876.

Schrebergärten

Leipzig is the birthplace of the allotment garden movement. These small urban plots with vegetables and flowers remain a beloved weekend tradition in eastern Germany.

Sächsischer Schlachteteller

A hearty Saxon meat plate with sliced smoked pork, sausages, Grünkohl (kale), and roasted potatoes — a staple of traditional Leipzig pubs.

Interesting Facts

  • Johann Sebastian Bach worked as Thomaskantor (choir director) in Leipzig for 27 years.
  • The Leipzig Trade Fair has run since the 12th century and remains an important economic event.
  • Leipzig is known for the "Leipzig School" of painting and a lively contemporary art scene.
  • The city’s nickname is "Hypezig" because of its growing cultural and creative industries.
  • Leipzig’s Schreberverein, founded in 1864, is considered the world’s first official allotment association.
